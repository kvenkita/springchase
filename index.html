About the website

Spring Chase is a free travel planning tool that helps you find the ideal time to visit any US state based on weather conditions. The target conditions — daytime highs up to 85°F with overnight lows in the 50s — were chosen to match what April in North Carolina feels like: warm but not oppressive, with crisp evenings perfect for being outdoors.

It's designed for anyone who travels with the weather in mind — motorcycle riders, road-trippers, hikers, campers, RV travelers, or anyone who'd rather not show up somewhere two weeks too early or too late in the season.

Each state's spring window is the approximate 30-day period when average daytime highs are at or below 85°F and average nighttime lows are in the 50s°F — the sweet spot before summer heat sets in. The window represents the start and end of that comfortable shoulder season, not a guarantee that every single day will hit those numbers.

Some states have a narrow window (Arizona's desert climbs quickly), while others hold comfortable conditions for longer (Pacific Northwest). The 30-day duration is a standardized estimate for comparison.

Methodology & data

Windows are derived from NOAA 30-year Climate Normals (1991–2020), which represent long-term monthly temperature averages for hundreds of US weather stations. These normals are published by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and are the standard reference used by meteorologists for climatological comparisons.

The data represents typical conditions over three decades — not a specific year's forecast, and not real-time weather. Think of it as a reliable baseline for what a given location feels like in a given month, averaged over a generation of measurements.

Using a single city per state can be misleading — Washington state's Seattle (coastal, mild) and Spokane (inland, continental) differ by nearly three weeks in their spring window. To capture this variation, four cities were selected per state to represent geographic spread: typically one each from the north, south, east, and west of the state, or from distinct climate zones (e.g. valley vs. mountain, coastal vs. inland).

The four city windows are then averaged to produce a single representative state window. You can see all four individual city windows in the tooltip when you hover over any state on the map or any row in the timeline.

The windows are accurate for general macro planning — they reliably tell you which month and approximate week a state hits peak spring conditions in an average year. They are not forecasts and will not tell you what next Tuesday will feel like.

Year-to-year variation is real: a given state's window might shift 1–3 weeks earlier or later in any specific year due to La Niña, El Niño, or other patterns. Always check actual forecasts (Weather.gov, Weather Underground, Windy) in the week before departure. Use Spring Chase to decide when to plan; use a live forecast to confirm the specific days.

Each state's window starts with NOAA 30-year Climate Normals (1991–2020) — monthly average high and low temperatures for hundreds of US weather stations. For each of the four sampled cities, the spring window start is the approximate day when the average high first falls at or below 85 °F and the average overnight low enters the 50s. Because NOAA publishes monthly data, the exact day is estimated by linear interpolation between adjacent months.

The four city start days are averaged (Math.round of the mean) to produce the state's avgDoy. The window is then fixed at 30 days from that start — a standardized duration that allows all 50 states to be compared on the same scale. The Route Planner finds matches using a standard interval overlap test: a state qualifies if any part of its 30-day window falls within your trip dates. Overlap percentage is overlap_days / 30 — the fraction of the state's peak spring captured by your trip.

How to navigate

The timeline shows all 50 states as horizontal bars against a full-year calendar. Each bar represents that state's 30-day spring window. The thin amber vertical line marks today's date.

Use the Region filter to narrow down to a part of the country, or Sort to reorder by date, alphabetically, or by region. The search box lets you jump to a specific state. Hover any row to see the 4-city breakdown behind that state's averaged window. States with a NOW badge are currently in their peak window.

The map in the Route Planner section updates in real time as you adjust your start date and trip length. States that have any overlap with your travel window light up in their region color; states outside your window dim to near-black.

Hover any state to see a tooltip with the state's averaged spring window, how many days of overlap it has with your trip, and the individual city breakdown. On mobile, tap a state to trigger the tooltip — it will disappear after a few seconds.

Alaska and Hawaii are positioned in their conventional map locations in the bottom-left corner.

Planning trips — motorcycle & car

Example — 4-week spring chase from the South to the Rockies: Set your start date to April 1 and trip length to 30 days. The map will highlight the Southeast, mid-Atlantic, Midwest, and lower Mountain West states all simultaneously in window. This gives you a menu of states to string together into a route heading north or northwest.

A practical loop might be: NC → Virginia → West Virginia → Kentucky → Missouri → Kansas → Colorado — all of which hit their spring windows within that stretch. By the time you reach Denver in late April, you're catching the tail of its comfortable window before summer dryness sets in.

Pro tip: set trip length to 60–90 days to see the full northward migration and plan a season-long tour that stays in spring the entire time.

Example — 2-week national parks run in May: Set start date to May 10 and trip length to 14 days. You'll see Utah, Colorado, Idaho, and the Pacific Northwest lighting up — perfect timing for Arches, Capitol Reef, or a Cascades loop without summer crowds or desert heat.

For a Southern fall alternative (if you missed spring), flip the logic: the Southeast states hit spring-like temperatures again in October–November on their way back down. The tool currently maps spring entry; the same data can inform fall planning by looking at the end of the warm season rather than the start.

Car travelers can use the overlap percentage bar in each state card to prioritize stops — a state with 90% overlap means almost your entire visit lands in peak conditions.

Caveats & things to watch out for

It's averages, not forecasts. A state can be "in window" per the data and still have a cold snap, rain system, or late frost. Always verify with a live weather service before committing to a departure.

Elevation matters enormously and is only partially captured by the 4-city sampling. A mountain pass in Colorado or a high-elevation meadow in the Sierras may be 2–4 weeks behind the city averages — or closed entirely. Check road conditions separately for high-elevation routes.

Hawaii's lows don't quite hit the 50s at sea level — overnight temps tend to stay in the mid-60s year-round. It's flagged with ⚠ in the data. Similarly, Alaska's interior (Fairbanks) approaches but doesn't reliably reach 50°F overnight in the summer window shown.

Climate change is shifting these windows. The NOAA 1991–2020 normals are the most current published dataset, but observed windows are already trending 1–2 weeks earlier in many states compared to older normals.

The windows represent averaged statewide conditions across 4 cities — your local area may peak earlier or later depending on your specific elevation, distance from coast, or urban heat island effect. A cyclist in coastal San Diego will experience spring 6+ weeks before someone in the Sierra foothills of the same state.

If your local experience consistently diverges from the mapped window, trust your experience for local trips. The tool is most useful for planning travel to unfamiliar states where you don't have that local intuition yet.

Feedback on specific windows that seem off is always welcome — hover the tooltip to see which 4 cities were sampled, and if the selection seems unrepresentative for your region, that's useful signal.

Methodology

How the windows
are computed

The full data pipeline — from NOAA climate normals to the day-of-year spring window shown for each state.

1. The spring benchmark

The target condition is defined as average daytime high ≤ 85 °F and average overnight low in the 50s °F (50–59 °F). These thresholds were chosen to match what April in North Carolina feels like — warm enough to be outdoors comfortably all day, cool enough at night to sleep with the windows open. North Carolina is the reference state (marked ★) and its window (approximately March 31 – April 30, averaging Charlotte, Raleigh, Asheville, and Wilmington) anchors the benchmark.

The 85 °F high ceiling marks the onset of oppressive summer heat in most of the country. The 50s low criterion captures the "crisp evening" quality of true spring — cool enough to feel seasonal, but not cold enough to require heavy gear overnight.

2. Data source

All temperature data comes from the NOAA U.S. Climate Normals (1991–2020), published by the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI). These normals report monthly average maximum and minimum temperatures for hundreds of weather stations across the United States. A 30-year period dampens single-year anomalies and provides a stable long-term baseline — these figures represent what a location typically experiences in a given month, averaged over a generation of measurements. The 1991–2020 period is the current official normal, updated every decade.

3. Translating monthly normals to a start day

NOAA normals are reported by month, not by day. Converting to a specific day-of-year start involves three steps:

Step 1 — Identify the threshold month

Scan monthly average highs and lows to find the first month where the high is at or below 85 °F and the overnight low has entered the 50s. The month-pair that brackets the transition (e.g. March still below threshold, April above) defines the target zone.

Step 2 — Interpolate to a day within the month

Monthly averages represent the midpoint of that month. Linear interpolation between adjacent months' midpoints estimates the specific day:

frac      = (threshold − prev_month_avg) / (next_month_avg − prev_month_avg)
start_day = mid_of_prev_month + frac × days_between_midpoints

This yields a fractional day-of-year, rounded to the nearest integer to produce startDoy for that city. Example — Charlotte, NC: March avg high ≈ 64 °F, avg low ≈ 44 °F (low not yet in 50s); April avg high ≈ 74 °F, avg low ≈ 54 °F (both criteria met). Interpolation places the crossing around March 31, giving Charlotte startDoy = 91.

Step 3 — Use the later of the two threshold crossings

The start day is the later of when the high crosses below 85 °F and when the low crosses above 50 °F. In practice the low criterion typically lags by days to a couple of weeks, making it the binding constraint in most states.

4. The 30-day window

Each city's spring window is fixed at 30 days starting from startDoy, spanning [startDoy, startDoy + 30] inclusive. This standardized duration enables visual comparison across states — it is not a precise forecast of exactly when conditions shift. In reality some states hold ideal conditions for 5–7 weeks; others (desert states) have a narrower window before heat arrives.

5. City selection

A single city per state can misrepresent the whole — Seattle (coastal, maritime) reaches spring conditions nearly three weeks later than Spokane (inland, continental), both in Washington. To capture intrastate variation, four cities are selected per state, representing distinct climate zones or geographic quadrants: typically N/S/E/W, or coastal vs. inland, valley vs. mountain.

6. State-level averaging

The state's avgDoy is the arithmetic mean of the four city start days, rounded to the nearest integer:

avgDoy = Math.round((city1 + city2 + city3 + city4) / 4)

Math.round(90.25) = 90. Displayed window: Mar 31 – Apr 30.

7. Day-of-year convention

The tool uses a day-of-year integer throughout: January 1 = 1, December 31 = 365. Today's DOY is computed from the system clock:

function doy() {
  const n = new Date(), s = new Date(n.getFullYear(), 0, 0);
  return Math.floor((n - s) / 86400000);
}

fmt(d) converts a DOY integer to a human-readable date (e.g. fmt(90) → "Mar 31") by constructing a Date anchored to January 2025 (a non-leap year). The ±1 day discrepancy in leap years is negligible for planning purposes.

8. Route Planner overlap logic

A state qualifies as a match if its spring window overlaps at all with the user's trip — standard interval intersection:

// state: [sd, sd+30]   trip: [pStart, pStart+pLen-1]
qualifies if: sd <= (pStart+pLen-1) && (sd+30) >= pStart

The number of overlapping days and the percentage shown in each match card:

overlapDays = Math.min(sd+30, pStart+pLen-1) - Math.max(sd, pStart) + 1
pct         = Math.round(overlapDays / 30 * 100)

The +1 makes both endpoints inclusive. The percentage is what fraction of the state's 30-day peak spring the trip captures.

9. State-specific caveats

Hawaii ⚠ — Sea-level overnight lows stay in the mid-60s year-round. The 50–59 °F low criterion is never strictly met at typical elevations. Window is included based on the high criterion alone.

Alaska ⚠ — Computed for Anchorage, Juneau, Kodiak, and Fairbanks. The Fairbanks interior doesn't reliably reach 50 °F overnight. Latest window of all 50 states (~day 152, early June).

Arizona — Largest intrastate spread: Phoenix (day 46, mid-Feb) vs. Flagstaff (day 121, May 1) differ by 75 days due to elevation. The four-city average (day 64, early March) accurately describes no single part of the state.

North Carolina ★ — The reference benchmark state. Its window was used to define the spring conditions criterion.

Washington — Seattle (day 145) and Spokane (day 130) differ by 15 days due to the Olympic rain shadow and contrasting maritime vs. continental climates.

10. Limitations

Averages, not forecasts. Any given year may deviate 1–3 weeks due to El Niño, La Niña, or other interannual variability. Always verify with a live forecast before departure.

Elevation is undersampled. A mountain pass at 3,500 m may be 3–4 weeks behind city averages. Check road conditions separately for high-elevation routes.

Interpolation error. Converting monthly normals to a start day introduces ±3–10 day error depending on the city's temperature curve shape.

Climate change. The 1991–2020 normals reflect ongoing warming trends. Windows are trending 1–2 weeks earlier than the prior 1981–2010 normals and this shift is continuing.

Fixed 30-day window. Some states hold ideal conditions for 5–7 weeks; others have a narrower window. The standardized 30-day duration enables comparison but can overstate or understate local reality.
